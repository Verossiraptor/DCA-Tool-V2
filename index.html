VIX — Market Fear Gauge

context only
VIX does not modify your multiplier in this tool. The backtest showed that pure drawdown tiers are the empirical driver of outperformance — the DD+VIX variant delivered $2.69M vs $3.14M for drawdown-only, meaning the VIX hedge cost ~$450K in final value. VIX is shown here as context: a high VIX during a drawdown confirms the fear is real, not a quiet orderly dip. Historically, VIX 40+ (GFC, COVID) has coincided with the best long-term entry points in the index's history.
Calm (<15)Unusually quiet. A drawdown here may be an orderly sell-off rather than capitulation.
Normal (15–20)Typical range. No additional signal from fear.
Elevated (20–30)Rising anxiety. Investors are pricing in uncertainty. Drawdown buys here tend to be better quality.
High Fear (30–40)Significant market stress. Historically near major dislocations. This is what the strategy is built for.
Panic (40+)Extreme fear — comparable to GFC (VIX 59) and COVID (VIX 53). The backtest's best entry points were here. Follow the tier. Buy.

Shiller CAPE Ratio
—
—

context only
CAPE does not modify your multiplier. The backtest started in January 2000 with CAPE at 43.77 — near today's level of ~39 — and still produced a 4.80x wealth multiple over 26 years. CAPE is useful as a long-horizon warning: high readings have historically meant lower forward 10-year returns. But the dot-com crash that followed CAPE 44 in 2000 ultimately created the buying opportunity that drove most of the strategy's outperformance. High CAPE signals caution about long-run expectations, not a reason to stop the strategy.
Cheap (<20)Below historical average (~17). Maximum long-run return potential.
Fair (20–25)Reasonable by modern standards. Normal forward return expectations.
Pricey (25–30)Above historical norm. Expect somewhat muted forward returns.
Expensive (30–35)Historically elevated. Lower expected 10-year returns. Strategy still works — buy the drawdowns.
Very Expensive (35+)Rare territory. Jan 2000 CAPE was 43.77. Today ~39. The strategy was backtested from this level. Compressed return expectations — but the crashes that follow expensive markets are exactly when this strategy shines.
